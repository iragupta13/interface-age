01

State of the Field

Neurotech is not one thing. It is a family of technologies that read neural activity, write back to nervous tissue, or close the loop between sensing and stimulation.

In 2026 the most honest summary is this: the field has definitely crossed the threshold from pure lab spectacle into early product reality, but it is still far from mature. We can already get useful outputs from the nervous system and can already change symptoms or behavior through stimulation in real humans[1][2][3].

What we do not yet have is a settled, mass-deployable neural product stack. The frontier is no longer just whether signals or therapies are possible. It is whether they can be durable, safe, trainable, reimbursable, maintainable, and trusted over years.

The Endgame

The deepest point of neurotech is not to make minds legible for entertainment. It is to restore function, reduce suffering, and eventually create trustworthy interfaces between biology and software.

The real tension is that the same tools that could make severe disability less isolating could also turn cognition, mood, or intent into product surfaces. That is why governance cannot be an afterthought.

The practical goal

Restore communication, movement, sensory function, or disease control so effectively that some of today’s hardest neurological barriers stop being permanent.

The platform goal

Build neural systems that work outside the lab, survive time, integrate into real care, and remain usable even as companies, models, and hardware generations change.

The governance goal

Prevent neural data from becoming the most intimate and least protected commercial data layer ever created.
